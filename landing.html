The Lead · On the Fund

Teenagers, managing real capital, doing the work of investors.

By the Members, for the Members

The Griffin Fund is the investment group of Grace Church School — a student-run, faculty-advised organization that researches public equities, votes on real positions, and manages a real portfolio. Pitches are prepared in industry pods, delivered to the general body, and taken to a weighted vote. Outcomes are tracked. Everyone is accountable.

What began as a speaker series has become a working research shop: a live portfolio with per-lot cost basis, a weighted voting chamber, a coverage library indexed by ticker, and a risk panel that reads Sharpe, beta, and drawdown like a professional desk. The following pages are a public-facing record of what the club has built and what it has done with the capital entrusted to it.

§ II · The Method

How a pitch becomes a position.

fig. 02
I.

Coverage by pod

Every sector of the market lives inside a pod of three to five analysts led by a senior portfolio manager. Pods meet privately, decide what to research, divide the work, and present together. This is how a high-school club begins to look like a sell-side desk.

Tech Consumer Financials Industrials +3
II.

The pitch & the vote

Pitches are delivered in front of the general body. A weighted voting session opens automatically; the President's ballots and the winning general-body opinion are tallied together. Every member is expected to cast an informed vote — Buy, Hold, or Sell — with a note.

III.

Real money, real record

Every approved pitch becomes a tracked position. The portfolio page shows live quotes, sector exposure, and risk (Sharpe, beta, drawdown). The coverage page keeps a ledger of every call the club has made: what we bought, what we passed on, and how each of them performed.

§ IV · The Instruments

Six rooms, one building.

fig. 04
№ 01

A live portfolio page that reads like a Bloomberg terminal.

Positions, weightings, sector exposure, Sharpe, annualized volatility, max drawdown, weighted beta, a 1W–1Y return curve. Quotes refresh on every load. Every member sees exactly what the portfolio manager sees.

№ 02

A voting chamber.

Every pitch opens a weighted session: the general body's winning ballot counts for two, the President's for one each. Tied sessions default to Hold. Every ballot carries a written reason.

№ 03

A coverage ledger.

Every ticker the club has researched, the verdict, the return since — indexed, searchable, and tied to the presenter who made the call.

№ 04

A risk panel.

For PMs and above: weighted β, annualized vol, max drawdown, HHI, and automatic rebalancing flags when a position or sector runs hot.

№ 05

Industry pods.

Seven sector pods, each with a leader, members, and a private chat. Pitches are tagged to the pod that owns the coverage.

№ 06

An attendance ledger and a research library.

Every meeting tracked, every report archived. The club runs on a record, not on memory — and the record is visible to everyone who belongs to it.

§ V · Governance

Who answers for it.

fig. 05

A hierarchy of accountability, not of privilege.

The President oversees the Fund. The Chief Investment Officer is responsible for the portfolio. Senior Portfolio Managers lead their industry pods and the analysts inside them. Every action that touches real capital — a pitch scheduled, a vote opened, a position recorded — is logged to an audit trail that cannot be edited after the fact.

Faculty advisors sit on the board as observers. They do not vote. They are there to ensure the Fund is run responsibly, as an educational organization, with the long-term interests of its members in mind.
